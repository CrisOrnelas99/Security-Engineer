#  What I learned – Introduction to Security Engineering

## Overview
This room introduces the **Security Engineer role** and explores the day-to-day responsibilities involved in protecting an organization's digital assets. It explains why organizations need security engineers, what qualifications are expected, and how they contribute to maintaining and improving a company’s security posture.

---

#  Why Do Organizations Need Security?

As organizations rely more on digital systems, they face increasing risks such as:

- Data breaches  
- Ransomware attacks  
- Service disruptions  
- Regulatory penalties  

Just as companies protect physical assets, they must also secure digital assets to ensure uninterrupted business operations. Security enables organizations to innovate while minimizing cyber risk.

---

#  The Role of a Security Engineer

A Security Engineer is responsible for:

- Owning the overall security posture of the organization  
- Minimizing cybersecurity risks  
- Designing secure systems and architectures  
- Conducting testing and identifying weaknesses  
- Implementing mitigation strategies  
- Collaborating across teams to enforce security standards  

Security Engineers break large security problems into manageable parts and solve them systematically.

---

#  Qualifications & Skills

Typical entry expectations include:

- 0–2 years of IT, networking, helpdesk, or security operations experience  
- Understanding of:
  - Computer networks  
  - Operating systems  
  - Basic programming  
  - Governance, Risk & Compliance (GRC) concepts  

The role is broad and varies between organizations.

---

#  Core Responsibilities

##  Asset Management
- Maintain inventory of all digital assets  
- Track IP addresses, applications, permissions, owners  
- Ensure assets are documented and updated  

> You can’t secure what you don’t know exists.

---

##  Security Policies
- Develop policies based on security principles  
- Enforce policy implementation  
- Evaluate and manage policy exceptions  
- Provide risk mitigation guidance  

---

##  Secure by Design
- Implement secure network architecture  
- Harden Windows, Linux, and Active Directory systems  
- Support Secure Software Development Lifecycle (SSDLC)  

Security should be built in from the beginning — not added later.

---

##  Security Assessment & Assurance
- Conduct audits and security assessments  
- Support red and purple team exercises  
- Prioritize and remediate findings  
- Continuously improve defenses  

Security is an ongoing process, not a one-time setup.

---

#  Continuous Responsibilities

##  Security Awareness
- Conduct training sessions  
- Reduce social engineering risks  
- Promote secure development and operational practices  

---

##  Risk Management
- Identify risks  
- Evaluate likelihood and impact  
- Recommend mitigation strategies  
- Advise leadership on risk acceptance decisions  

> Not all risks can be eliminated — some must be mitigated or accepted strategically.

---

##  Change Management
- Evaluate security impact of system updates  
- Ensure new integrations meet security standards  
- Conduct risk assessments before deployment  

---

##  Vulnerability Management
- Monitor new vulnerabilities  
- Patch systems based on severity  
- Reduce exposure to evolving threats  

---

##  Compliance & Audits
- Maintain regulatory compliance (e.g., PCI-DSS, HIPAA, ISO, NIST)  
- Work with auditors  
- Maintain security certifications  

---

#  Additional Responsibilities

Depending on the organization, a Security Engineer may also:

- Manage security tools (SIEM, Firewalls, WAF, EDR)  
- Participate in procurement decisions  
- Conduct tabletop exercises  
- Assist in disaster recovery and business continuity planning  
- Support crisis management operations  

---

#  Business vs Security Balance

Security decisions must consider:

- Business operations  
- Cost  
- Usability  
- Implementation feasibility  

The most secure system is one that is powered off — but that achieves no business objective. Security Engineers must balance protection with productivity.

---

#  Key Takeaways

A Security Engineer:

- Owns the organization’s cybersecurity posture  
- Designs and maintains secure systems  
- Continuously improves defenses  
- Manages risk strategically  
- Supports incident response and crisis management  
- Collaborates across departments  

---

##  Final Reflection

Security engineering is not a one-time task but a continuous journey of improvement, adaptation, and risk management.
